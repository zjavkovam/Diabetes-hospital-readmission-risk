We’ve added outlier detection and replacement with the median for numerical columns in the dataset. The function detect_replace_outliers calculates quartiles and interquartile range to identify outliers, which are then replaced with the column’s median. Applied to all numerical columns using lapply, this ensures outliers are addressed uniformly. The dataset is then displayed to reflect the changes.

# Function to detect and replace outliers with median
detect_replace_outliers <- function(x) {
  # Calculate the first and third quartiles
  Q1 <- quantile(x, 0.25)
  Q3 <- quantile(x, 0.75)
  
  # Calculate the interquartile range (IQR)
  IQR <- Q3 - Q1
  
  # Define lower and upper bounds for outliers detection
  lower_bound <- Q1 - 1.5 * IQR
  upper_bound <- Q3 + 1.5 * IQR
  
  # Detect outliers
  outliers <- x < lower_bound | x > upper_bound
  
  # Replace outliers with median
  x[outliers] <- median(x, na.rm = TRUE)
  
  return(x)
}

Bayesian network

Selected variables for Bayesian network nodes:

  • Age: Age is an important factor in understanding the risk and progression of diabetes. It can provide insights into how diabetes management varies across different age groups.

  • Gender: Gender may influence various aspects of diabetes diagnosis, treatment, and outcomes. It’s important to explore potential gender-related differences in diabetes management.

  • Number of diagnosis: The number of diagnoses a patient has can indicate the complexity of their medical condition, which may impact diabetes management and outcomes.

  • Number of meds: The number of medications a patient is taking can reflect the severity of their condition and the complexity of their treatment regimen, which may influence diabetes outcomes.

  • A1C: A1C levels are crucial for monitoring and managing diabetes. High A1C levels indicate poor blood sugar control and increase the risk of complications.

  • Diabetes meds: Whether a patient is prescribed diabetes medication can indicate the severity of their condition and the approach to treatment.

  • Number of hospital visits: The frequency of hospital visits can provide insights into the healthcare utilization patterns of diabetic patients and may reflect disease severity.

  • Readmission risk: Predicting the risk of hospital readmission is important for healthcare planning and improving patient outcomes, especially for chronic conditions like diabetes.

  • Length of stay: The length of hospital stay can indicate the severity of acute episodes related to diabetes complications and may influence future healthcare needs.

With relationships:

The relationships between these variables can provide insights into how demographic factors (age, gender) interact with clinical factors (number of diagnosis, A1C, medication usage) and healthcare utilization (hospital visits, length of stay) to influence readmission risk.

  • For example, age and gender may influence A1C levels, which in turn may affect the choice of diabetes medication and subsequent readmission risk.

  • Understanding these relationships can help healthcare providers tailor interventions and improve outcomes for diabetic patients.

# Define conditional probability function
conditional_probability <- function(joint_table, marginal_table, condition_vars, event_var, event_value) {
  # Extract conditional probabilities from joint and marginal tables based on condition variables
  condition_prob <- 1
  for (var in names(condition_vars)) {
    condition_prob <- condition_prob * joint_table[joint_table[[var]] == condition_vars[[var]], 2] /
      marginal_table[marginal_table[[var]] == condition_vars[[var]], 2]
  }
  
  # Calculate conditional probability
  conditional_prob <- sum(condition_prob * joint_table[joint_table[[event_var]] == event_value, 2])
  
  return(conditional_prob)
}
